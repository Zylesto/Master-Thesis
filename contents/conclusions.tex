\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{chapter:conclusion}

This thesis proposed and implemented a multidimensional vulnerability classification and remediation framework to address existing shortcomings in the vulnerability management component of the \ac{SCA} Tool developed at the \ac{POS} of \ac{FAU}, particularly focusing on limitations observed with exclusive reliance on \ac{CVSS} scoring. By integrating empirical exploitability metrics from \ac{EPSS} with technical severity assessments from \ac{CVSS}, the developed model provides a more comprehensive, context-aware approach to prioritizing software vulnerabilities.

The conducted evaluation demonstrated that the developed system fully satisfies all functional and non-functional requirements, validating the effectiveness of integrating multiple scoring dimensions and stakeholder-specific remediation strategies. Domain experts confirmed the practical relevance and applicability of the models and algorithms, noting alignment with current industry best practices. Particularly positive was the combination of technical severity with real-world exploit probability, which significantly enhances prioritization efficiency and remediation precision.

However, the expert evaluation highlighted potential improvement areas. Specifically, merging \ac{CVSS} and \ac{EPSS} was debated due to their partial overlap; some experts advocated adjusting weighting to favor empirical data provided by \ac{EPSS}. Communication around missing data handling emerged as an area requiring clearer visual indicators or separate categorization to avoid potential ambiguity in prioritization.

Additionally, expert feedback emphasized the importance of compliance considerations as integral to cybersecurity risk management and recommended further expansion of grouping capabilities, such as by business units or technology stacks, to increase practical usability.

In summary, the developed framework addresses critical vulnerabilities' prioritization challenges by merging standardized technical assessments with dynamic, empirical threat intelligence. Future research directions include refining placeholder handling for missing data, integrating compliance explicitly into decision-making processes, and enhancing usability through advanced vulnerability grouping options. These enhancements would further optimize vulnerability management processes, aligning them closer to organizational security strategies and operational effectiveness.
